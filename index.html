1 Hour of Smoke Is Enough for Real Barbecue

The Ballad of a Barbecue Rustler
Working Draft
1

LOW ANGLE — CHRIS

Chris's face fills the frame — low angle, anamorphic, ~50mm, shallow focus. A hand rests on the smoker lid; he looks straight down the lens. On his last word he lifts the lid — it rises into the bottom of frame; cut on the lift.

CHRIS

Great barbecue is supposed to take all day.

2

INSIDE THE SMOKER — MATCH ON THE LIFT

The rising lid wipes up through frame, half of it still black; as the lid clears, sunlight breaks through billowing smoke and an amazing-looking, almost-black shoulder snaps into focus. 3/4 top-down, gentle push in.

CHRIS (V.O.)

But this pork shoulder has only smoked for one hour—

3

THE SHOULDER LANDS

MATCH CUT — the same shoulder drops onto a table in front of a row of pitmasters.

CHRIS (V.O.)

—and it's good enough to serve to some of the best pitmasters in the West.

4

HIGH ANGLE — DOWN ON THE PITMASTERS

Looking down on the pitmasters at the table. They study the meat, then look up — to the lens.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Because if they can't tell I cheated—

5

WIDE — OVER THE PITMASTERS

From slightly behind the pitmasters — their shoulders dark in the foreground — looking across the table at Chris. He pats the smoke and dust off himself and starts to walk away; hard sunlight behind him backlights the smoke and dust as he goes. The Buster Scruggs walk-off.

CHRIS (V.O.)

—then you can leave all the smoke and sweat behind, and with only one hour of real effort, you can make some of the best barbecue in your town.
