1 Hour of Smoke Is Enough for Real Barbecue

A barbecue Western, in two registers · reverse-smoke pulled pork
Screenplay — Working Draft

Format note. The film runs in two registers, and the cut between them is the argument. [WESTERN] is the legend — sepia, mythic, where Chris is the outlaw "heretic" and the pitmasters are the law. [MODERN] is the truth — a clean present-day kitchen, where Chris narrates the real story and teaches the science. Modern Chris is telling the tale of his own legend; the cut from myth to lab is what punctures the myth.

— Cold Open: The Claim —

1

Extreme close — the bark[MODERN]

Black. Then EXTREME MACRO, slow motion: two fingers split a glistening, mahogany-black crust. It shatters like glass. Steam rolls out. The meat underneath gives way into wet, ropey strands — no resistance at all.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Real barbecue. One hour of smoke.

2

The strands fall apart. A single bead of rendered fat catches the light and drops.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Bark, smoke, falls apart in your fingers.

3

CUT TO: CHRIS, present day, to camera. Warm kitchen.

CHRIS

And a blind panel of the best pitmasters in the country couldn't tell it from a cook that took all day.

A beat.

CHRIS

They did not take that well.

4

Smash to — a saloon post[WESTERN]

A nail driven through paper. A sun-bleached WANTED poster slaps onto a weathered post. Hand-lettered, beneath a woodcut of our man:

WANTED — CHRIS YOUNG — FOR CHEATING BARBECUE. REWARD: THE HOURS OF YOUR LIFE BACK.

A lone harmonica sting. The title burns in over the poster.

TITLE: 1 HOUR OF SMOKE IS ENOUGH FOR REAL BARBECUE

— Act I: The Legend —

5

Ext. prairie — the all-night fire — night (sepia)[WESTERN]

Embers breathe in the dark. An old keeper of the flame — THE PITMASTER — feeds a split log into a firebox like an offering. Smoke climbs into a field of stars. It is genuinely beautiful. We honor it.

BALLAD (V.O.)

Gather 'round, and I'll tell you the tale / of the man that they hanged for his 'cue — / he slept like a babe while the embers turned pale / and swore that an hour would do…

6

The Pitmaster's face, lit orange. Sweat. Devotion. A whole town's worth of reverence in one tired man.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Everybody knows how real barbecue gets made. Low. Slow. All day and all night. A man, his fire, and the long, holy wait.

CHRIS (V.O.)

And the pitmasters are right about almost everything. The wood. The smoke. The patience.

Beat.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Almost.

7

A dusty street. Townsfolk lean together, watching our man pass. Whispers — planting the "sorcery" the science will later disarm.

TOWNSWOMAN

They say he's got a devil's needle that tells the future.

TOWNSMAN

They say he sleeps while his meat cooks.

TOWNSWOMAN

Ain't natural.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Me? I'm the one they came for. But let's start the night it all began — with the hardest thing I've ever done in barbecue.

8

Int. bedroom — night // ext. fire — night[CUT]

MODERN. A dark bedroom. Phone glow on CHRIS's face. He's scrolling a barbecue forum — "THE ONLY RIGHT WAY," "12 HOURS OR IT ISN'T REAL," a flame-war of replies. Lower-third clock: 11:47 PM.

CUT, sepia: the Pitmaster spritzes, squints into the smoke. 2:14 AM.

CUT, modern: Chris hasn't moved. Eyes half closed.

CUT, sepia: the Pitmaster fights a flare-up, arms up against the heat. 4:30 AM.

CUT, modern: the phone rests on Chris's chest. He is asleep. A soft snore. Through the doorway, the oven glows like a little hearth.

CHRIS (V.O.)

The night this pork shoulder cooked, I did the hardest thing in barbecue. I slept.

9

An alarm. Chris ambles into the kitchen in a robe, coffee in hand. The oven timer reads DONE.

CHRIS

Morning.

CHRIS (V.O.)

I'm the guy who invented the sous-vide machine. I've also done it the all-day way, plenty of times. Which is how I know the secret the legend really doesn't want you to hear.

— Act II: The Heresy —

10

Int. modern kitchen — day[MODERN]

JUNE — a sharp young cook, sleeves rolled, a notebook already open — leans on the counter. Chris sets the finished shoulder between them.

JUNE

Okay. Everyone says twelve hours, low and slow. You did one. So either everyone's wrong, or you're lying to me.

CHRIS

Third option. Everyone's right about the smoke — and wrong about the wait. Watch.

11

CHRIS

Barbecue "stalls." Hours where the meat just sits at one temperature and nothing seems to happen. People think that's the fat rendering. The magic.

JUNE

It's not?

CHRIS

It's sweat. The meat is a wet sponge, and it sweats water as fast as the fire pushes heat in. Almost all that energy goes into evaporating water — not cooking, not bark. The surface stalls out cool, and the inside can't get any hotter than the surface.

12

Inside the cooker — cutaway[MODERN / CGI]

Chris taps the lid of the Big Green Egg. MATCH-CUT into the impossible shot — the Egg sliced clean in half against glass. Real smoke curls up and around a cross-sectioned shoulder. We push in.

The surface is sheathed in a faint, shimmering haze — water, leaving. The smoke streams up and slides right past it. Nothing lands.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Here's what no one's ever shown you. While it's sweating, smoke can't stick. It just rides the steam away. You're burning wood to make fog.

Time moves. The haze thins. The surface dries, darkens, tightens — and now the smoke BITES: compounds settle and brown, bark blooming in fast-forward as the surface climbs from 140 toward 200°F.

CHRIS (V.O.)

But the second the sweating stops, it reacts. Bark. In minutes, not hours.

13

A slim probe slides into the meat. On screen, a graph draws itself live: the SURFACE line flattens — the stall — while the CORE line crawls up underneath and kisses it, unable to pass. Then, as the water runs out, both climb together.

JUNE

And that's your... future-telling needle.

CHRIS

A predictive thermometer. It reads the surface — the thing that actually controls the bark — and tells me where it's headed. The town thinks it's witchcraft.

He grins.

CHRIS

It's just looking at the right thing.

14

Ext. modern yard — day[MODERN]

Chris stands between two piles: a heaping wheelbarrow of split oak, and a small handful of chunks.

CHRIS

This is the wood an all-day cook burns. And here's what most of it actually does.

A sweating shoulder billows steam; a whole wheelbarrow's worth of smoke just floats up and away into nothing.

CHRIS

It boils off water. You're paying a cord of wood to wring out a sponge.

15

A raw shoulder on a scale — a number. Then the same shoulder after its overnight — the number drops, hard.

CHRIS

This shoulder lost almost a fifth of its weight in water.

Beat.

CHRIS

While I slept. No fire. No smoke wasted. Just time I wasn't awake for.

16

Int. modern kitchen — night[MODERN]

CHRIS

So here's the trick they'd hang me for. Split the job in two.

He holds up an immersion circulator — his own invention — considers it a moment, then sets it gently aside with a little smile.

CHRIS

You don't even need the thing I invented. Wrap the shoulder in foil, low oven, overnight. It pre-pays all that water loss while you sleep —

JUNE

— so by morning the hard part's already done.

CHRIS

Already done. All that's left is the part that was ever worth doing.

17

Morning. Chris lifts the foil pouch and pours — a startling amount of cloudy liquid streams into a clear measuring cup. June's eyebrows climb.

CHRIS

That's what you'd have spent all day — and a face full of smoke — sweating out over a fire. I did it overnight, in my sleep.

He sets the cup down, an aside.

CHRIS

Comes out juicier this way, too. But that's another video.

18

Ext. patio — the egg — day[MODERN]

Cold-start smoke; thin and blue. A clock sits in frame. TIMELAPSE: the dewatered shoulder darkening, the probe climbing 140 → 200°F, bark forming — in one hour.

CHRIS (V.O.)

One hour of smoke. On a surface that's finally ready for it.

19

CHRIS

But you shouldn't take my word for it. And neither should they.

JUNE

Who's "they"?

A small smile.

CHRIS

The people who'd know if I was lying.

BALLAD (V.O.)

So he carried his heresy into town…

— Act III: The Showdown —

20

Int. saloon — day (sepia)[WESTERN]

Batwing doors. CHRIS — now the outlaw, duster, the predictive thermometer worn at his hip like a sidearm. The player piano stops dead. At a green-felt table sit four or five famous PITMASTERS — the legendary best — with THE PITMASTER (our foil) at the head.

BALLAD (V.O.)

…where the kings of the coals sat waiting.

21

CHRIS

Gentlemen. A friendly game.

He lays down a wad of bills. Then three covered plates.

CHRIS

Three plates. Some all-day. Some one-hour. Tell me which is which. You're the best in the world — should be easy money.

THE PITMASTER

Son, I can taste a shortcut from across the room.

CHRIS

I'm counting on it.

22

He lifts the covers, shuffles the plates three-card-monte quick, re-covers. Forks hover. The saloon holds its breath. Then Chris steps back — out the door entirely. No face to read, no tell to catch.

Note: under the showmanship is a real triangle test — two plates of one cook, one of the other; pick the odd one out. The theater is the monte; the rigor is the method.

23

Silence. Chewing. Furrowed brows. One pitmaster nods to himself, certain. THE PITMASTER sets his fork down and taps a plate.

THE PITMASTER

This one. You can taste the time in it. That's your all-day.

24

Chris turns the little tag on the plate. A beat held a half-second too long.

CHRIS

That's the one-hour.

The table goes silent. A chalkboard tallies the verdict: THE PROS COULDN'T TELL. Bills slide across the felt to Chris. The Pitmaster's certainty curdles into something colder.

25

At the bar, JUNE — taught the mechanism that very morning — quietly slides her own pick forward. She's right. She catches Chris's eye.

CHRIS (V.O.)

The girl I'd taught at breakfast read it clean. The men who'd given their lives to the fire could not.

Beat.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Understanding beats experience. That's the part they couldn't forgive.

26

Chris fans the bills out like a winning hand. MATCH-CUT to the graphic: a twelve-hour bar — eleven hours gray, a meter reading "evaporating water... evaporating water..." — then one thin glowing slice at the end labeled "bark."

CHRIS (V.O.)

All day — and almost all of it was water leaving. The bark, the part you can actually taste, happens in the last hour. I just stopped doing the other eleven.

— Act IV: Frontier Justice —

27

Int. saloon — continuous (sepia)[WESTERN]

THE PITMASTER stands, knocking his chair back. "Cheat." The word travels the room. The pitmasters rise. A lawman's hand settles on Chris's shoulder.

CHRIS (V.O.)

The pitmasters? They did not take it well.

28

Int. modern kitchen — day[MODERN]

HARD CUT to clean, warm, present day. Chris plates the pulled pork and talks straight to us. No bit. No duster.

CHRIS

Here's what I actually want you to take from this. The suffering was never the thing. The all-night vigil, the burned wood, the aching back — it felt like devotion. Most of it was just boiling water.

CHRIS

The hour that mattered was always small. And you can do that hour — in your backyard, on a weeknight, after a full night's sleep.

He pulls the bark apart again — the money shot returns.

CHRIS

And honestly? It's not just barbecue. How many things do we do the hard way just because the hard way feels holy?

He slides a plate forward — toward us.

CHRIS

Eat. You earned it. Even if you didn't suffer for it.

— The Stinger —

29

Int. frontier courtroom — day (sepia)[WESTERN / AI]

CHRIS (V.O.)

Course... the legend doesn't end so kind.

A dusty courtroom. Chris, hands bound, hauled before a TOWN JUDGE by the humiliated pitmasters. Their spokesman — the Foil — points a finger.

THE PITMASTER

He was caught cheating barbecue.

The judge barely looks up. A beat.

JUDGE

Good enough. Hang him.

Gavel.

30

Ext. gallows — day (sepia)[WESTERN / AI]

A noose settles around Chris's neck. Beside him, a YOUNG MAN weeps, terrified. Chris glances over, deadpan.

CHRIS

First time?

Chris's eyes lift to the crowd — and find JUNE. She looks up at him. A held beat: she knows now. The idea is loose in the world, and no rope can reach it.

A hood comes down over his head.

BALLAD (V.O.)

They hanged the poor man... but the recipe got away.

CUT TO BLACK. The trap drops — sound only.

END — 30 SHOTS
