1 Hour of Smoke Is Enough for Real Barbecue

A barbecue Western, in two registers · reverse-smoke pulled pork
Screenplay — Working Draft

Format note. Two registers, and the cut between them is the argument. [WESTERN] is the legend — sepia, mythic; Chris is the town's uncanny INVENTOR (a Tesla in the Old West), condemned for "witchcraft against barbecue," and the pitmasters are the law. [MODERN] is the truth — a clean present-day kitchen where Chris narrates and teaches the science. A cold electric-BLUE light marks Chris's inventions wherever they appear, in both eras (and at the red-pill threshold), tying the worlds into one. Modern Chris narrates his own legend; the cut from myth to lab is what punctures the myth. Comic engine throughout: cosmic dread meeting an engineer's shrug.

— Cold Open: The Claim —

1

Extreme close — the bark[MODERN]

Black. Then EXTREME MACRO, slow motion: two fingers split a glistening, mahogany-black crust. It shatters like glass. Steam rolls out. The meat underneath gives way into wet, ropey strands — no resistance at all.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Real barbecue. One hour of smoke — not twelve.

2

The strands fall apart. A single bead of rendered fat catches the light and drops.

CHRIS (V.O.)

And the best pitmasters in the country couldn't tell it from a cook that took all day.

3

CUT TO: CHRIS, present day, to camera. Warm kitchen.

CHRIS

Here's the cheat. The long part — the all-night part — I do it in my sleep. In the oven. The fire only touches it for one hour.

CHRIS

That hour was the only part that was ever doing anything.

A beat.

CHRIS

They did not take that well.

4

Smash to — a saloon post[WESTERN]

A nail driven through paper. A sun-bleached WANTED poster slaps onto a weathered post, beneath a woodcut of our man:

WANTED — CHRIS YOUNG — FOR WITCHCRAFT AGAINST BARBECUE

Sorcery · Unnatural devices · Sleeping while the meat cooks

A lone harmonica sting. The title burns in over the poster.

TITLE: 1 HOUR OF SMOKE IS ENOUGH FOR REAL BARBECUE

— Act I: The Legend —

5

Ext. prairie — the all-night fire — night (sepia)[WESTERN]

Embers breathe in the dark. An old keeper of the flame — THE PITMASTER — feeds a split log into a firebox like an offering. Smoke climbs into a field of stars. It is genuinely beautiful. We honor it. (One-second hard cut to MODERN — the bark pull — then back. The grammar is set in the first Western beat: sepia = legend, kitchen = truth.)

BALLAD (V.O.)

Gather 'round, and I'll tell you the tale / of the man that they hanged for his 'cue — / he slept like a babe while the embers turned pale / and swore that an hour would do…

CHRIS (V.O.)

Everybody knows how real barbecue gets made. Low. Slow. All day and all night. And the pitmasters are right about almost everything — the wood, the smoke, the patience. Almost.

6

Ext. edge of town — the inventor's lab — night (sepia)[WESTERN]

A clapboard building at the end of Main. Cold electric-BLUE light leaks through the slats; a faint hum. A hand-painted shingle: "C. YOUNG — DEVICES." Townsfolk cross the street to avoid it. They whisper.

TOWNSWOMAN

They say he's got a needle that tells the future.

TOWNSMAN

They say he cut a cooker clean in half — and it still drew breath.

TOWNSWOMAN

Built a machine to do his waiting for him.

TOWNSMAN

A man's supposed to suffer his meat. He engineers it.

TOWNSWOMAN

Ain't natural.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Me? I'm the one they came for.

7

Int. bedroom — night // ext. fire — night[INTERCUT W/M]

MODERN: a dark bedroom, phone glow on CHRIS. He's scrolling a barbecue forum — "THE ONLY RIGHT WAY," "12 HOURS OR IT ISN'T REAL," "HE SLEEPS WHILE HIS MEAT COOKS." Clock: 11:47 PM.

CUT, sepia: the Pitmaster spritzes, squints into the smoke. 2:14 AM.

CUT, modern: Chris hasn't moved.

CUT, sepia: the Pitmaster fights a flare-up, arms up against the heat. 4:30 AM.

CUT, modern: the phone rests on Chris's chest. He is asleep. A soft snore. Through the doorway, the oven glows like a little hearth.

CHRIS (V.O.)

The night this pork shoulder cooked, I did the hardest thing in barbecue. I slept.

8

Int. modern kitchen — morning[MODERN]

An alarm. Chris ambles into the kitchen in a robe, coffee in hand. The oven timer reads DONE.

CHRIS

Morning.

CHRIS (V.O.)

I helped build the sous-vide circulator a lot of you own. I've done barbecue the all-day way, too. Which is how I know the secret the legend really doesn't want you to hear.

— Act II: The Heresy —

9

Int. modern kitchen — day[MODERN]

JESSE — a sharp young cook, sleeves rolled, a notebook already open — leans on the counter. Chris sets the finished shoulder between them.

JESSE

My grandfather cooked sixteen hours. Every Sunday of his life. You're telling me he wasted most of it?

CHRIS

I'm telling you he was right about the smoke — and wrong about the wait. But I can't just tell you. You have to choose to see it.

10

Int. modern kitchen — the offer[MODERN]

The kitchen drops to a single hard, cold-BLUE source — the same light as the lab. Chris slides on round, mirrored glasses. Two pills in his open palms: blue, red.

CHRIS

Blue pill — you go home, light the fire at dawn, tend it sixteen hours, and believe every one of them made it better.

CHRIS

Red pill — I show you where the time actually goes. And you can never un-taste it.

A long beat. Jesse takes the red pill. The blue light snaps back to a clean, bright kitchen.

CHRIS (V.O.)

All I'm offering is the truth. About barbecue.

11

CHRIS

Barbecue "stalls" — hours where the temperature just sits and nothing seems to happen. People think that's the fat rendering. The magic.

JESSE

It's not?

CHRIS

It's sweat. The meat's a wet sponge, and almost all the fire's heat goes into boiling water off the surface — not into the meat. So the surface gets stuck at the temperature of wet skin, and the inside can't outrun it. It's a ceiling.

Beat.

CHRIS

Dry the surface, and the ceiling lifts.

12

Inside the cooker — cutaway[MODERN / CGI]

Chris taps the lid of the Big Green Egg. MATCH-CUT into the impossible shot — the Egg sliced clean in half against glass. Real smoke curls up and around a cross-sectioned shoulder.

CHRIS (V.O.)

In the legend, they said I cut a smoker in half and it kept on smoking. They weren't wrong. Here's the part they couldn't watch.

We push in. The surface is sheathed in a shimmering haze — water, leaving. The smoke streams up and slides right past it. Nothing lands.

CHRIS (V.O.)

While it's sweating, the smoke can't land — the water pushing out carries it right back off. All that wood, and almost none of the flavor sticks.

Time moves. The haze thins. The surface — stuck in the 150s — dries, darkens, tightens, and now races up. The smoke BITES: compounds settle and brown, bark blooming as the surface climbs past 200°F.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Smoke isn't a soak. It's a reaction. The second the sweating stops, it reacts — bark, in minutes, not hours.

13

A slim probe slides into the meat. On screen, a graph draws itself live: the SURFACE line flattens — stuck low — while the CORE line crawls up underneath and kisses it, unable to pass. The flat stretch shades GRAY, labeled "evaporating water." Then the water runs out and both lines climb together, past 200°F.

JESSE

And that's your... future-telling needle.

CHRIS

A predictive thermometer. It reads the surface — the thing that actually controls the bark — and tells me where it's headed. The town thinks it's witchcraft.

He grins.

CHRIS

It's just looking at the right thing.

14

Ext. modern yard — day[MODERN]

Chris stands between two piles: a heaping wheelbarrow of split oak, and a small handful of chunks.

CHRIS

This whole wheelbarrow is the wood an all-day cook burns. And here's what most of it actually does.

A sweating shoulder billows steam; the wheelbarrow's worth of smoke floats up and away into nothing.

CHRIS

It boils off water. The handful is all the bark ever needed.

15

Int. modern kitchen — night[MODERN]

CHRIS

So here's the trick they'd hang me for. Split the job in two.

He holds up an immersion circulator — his own — considers it a moment, then sets it gently aside with a little smile.

CHRIS

You don't even need the thing I helped build. Wrap the shoulder in foil, low oven, overnight. It pre-pays all that water loss while you sleep —

JESSE

— so by morning the hard part's already done.

CHRIS

Already done.

Dry, to camera.

CHRIS

They'd have burned me for the machine. They'll burn me for this instead.

16

Morning. Chris lifts the foil pouch and pours — a startling amount of cloudy liquid streams into a clear measuring cup. Jesse's eyebrows climb.

CHRIS

I weighed it. That's a fifth of the shoulder's weight — water — gone while I slept. The same water an all-day fire spends all day boiling off. I just caught it in a cup.

CHRIS

And because that long, gentle hold turns the tough collagen to gelatin, it comes out juicier — not drier.

17

Int. modern kitchen — the recipe[MODERN]

ON SCREEN — "THE ACTUAL RECIPE" — building line by line as Chris talks it through, plain and unhurried.

CHRIS

Here's the whole thing, so you can actually make it.

1. Salt the shoulder, wrap it tight in foil.

2. Lowest your oven goes — about 170°F — overnight, until the surface holds around 160 and it's shed that fifth of its weight.

3. Chill it, so the surface firms up and dries.

4. Cold-start smoke, about an hour, until the surface climbs past 200 and the bark sets.

CHRIS

One night asleep. One hour of smoke. That's the whole cheat.

18

Ext. patio — the egg — day[MODERN]

Cold-start smoke; thin and blue. A clock sits in frame. TIMELAPSE: the dewatered shoulder darkening, the probe climbing toward 200°F, bark forming — in one hour.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Thin blue smoke, on a surface that's finally dry enough to take it. This is the hour that was always the point.

19

CHRIS

But don't take my word for it. And neither should they.

JESSE

Who's "they"?

A small smile.

CHRIS

The people who'd know if I was lying.

BALLAD (V.O.)

So he carried his heresy into town…

— Act III: The Showdown —

20

Int. saloon — day (sepia)[WESTERN]

Batwing doors. CHRIS — the outlaw inventor, duster, the predictive thermometer worn at his hip like a sidearm; it glows faint blue and clicks once in the silence. The player piano stops dead. A pitmaster knocks the wood table and spits — a warding gesture. At a green-felt table sit four or five famous PITMASTERS — the legendary best — THE PITMASTER (our foil) at the head.

BALLAD (V.O.)

…where the kings of the coals sat waiting.

21

CHRIS

Gentlemen. A friendly game.

He lays down a wad of bills. Then three covered plates.

CHRIS

Three plates. Some all-day. Some one-hour. Tell me which is which. You're the best in the world — should be easy money.

THE PITMASTER

Son, I can taste a shortcut from across the room.

CHRIS

I'm counting on it.

22

He lifts the covers and shows the room — and us — the setup plainly, so the test is visible, not hidden:

CHRIS

Two of these are the same cook. One's the odd one out. Find it.

He shuffles the three plates three-card-monte quick, re-covers, and steps back — out the door entirely. No face to read, no tell to catch. (The theater is the monte; the rigor is a real triangle test, and now the audience can see it's rigged for honesty, not for Chris.)

23

Silence. Chewing. Furrowed brows. One pitmaster nods to himself, certain. THE PITMASTER sets his fork down and taps a plate.

THE PITMASTER

This one. You can taste the time in it. That's your all-day.

24

Chris turns the little tag on the plate. A beat held a half-second too long.

CHRIS

That's the one-hour.

The table goes silent. A chalkboard tallies the verdict: THE PROS COULDN'T TELL. Bills slide across the felt to Chris. The Pitmaster's certainty curdles — not into a gambler's anger, but a congregation's dread. He crosses himself with a rib bone.

25

At the bar, JESSE sets down her own pick — the odd plate — and says it out loud, to the room.

JESSE

Plate two. Its surface never really dried — too even, all the way through. That's the all-day.

She's right.

CHRIS (V.O.)

She wasn't tasting harder than them. She knew there was nothing there to taste — so she ignored the one thing the legends trust most: that you can taste the hours.

Beat.

CHRIS (V.O.)

You can't.

26

The verdict, in numbers[MODERN]

Chris fans the bills out like a winning hand. MATCH-CUT: the gray flat stretch from the stall graph (shot 13) rotates and collapses into a twelve-hour bar — eleven hours gray, a meter reading "evaporating water... evaporating water..." — then one thin glowing slice at the end: "bark."

CHRIS (V.O.)

All day — and almost all of it was water leaving. The bark, the part you can actually taste, happens in the last hour. I just stopped doing the other eleven.

— Act IV: Frontier Justice —

27

Int. saloon — continuous (sepia)[WESTERN]

THE PITMASTER stands, knocking his chair back. "Witch." The word travels the room. The pitmasters rise like a congregation. A lawman's hand settles on Chris's shoulder.

CHRIS (V.O.)

The pitmasters? They did not take it well.

28

Int. modern kitchen — day[MODERN]

HARD CUT to clean, warm, present day. Chris plates the pulled pork and talks straight to us. No bit. No duster.

CHRIS

Here's what I actually want you to take from this. The suffering was never the thing. The all-night vigil, the burned wood, the aching back — it felt like devotion. Most of it was just boiling water.

CHRIS

The hour that mattered was always small. And you can do that hour — in your backyard, on a weeknight, after a full night's sleep.

He pulls the bark apart again — the money shot returns.

CHRIS

And honestly? It's not just barbecue. How many things do we do the hard way just because the hard way feels holy?

He slides a plate forward — toward us.

CHRIS

Eat. You earned it. Even if you didn't suffer for it.

— The Stinger —

29

Int. frontier courtroom — day (sepia)[WESTERN / AI]

CHRIS (V.O.)

Course... the legend doesn't end so kind.

A dusty courtroom. Chris, hands bound, hauled before a TOWN JUDGE by the humiliated pitmasters. Their spokesman — the Foil — points a finger.

THE PITMASTER

He builds machines to do a Christian's suffering for him. He's got a needle that knows the future. He cut a cooker clean in half — and it lived.

The judge barely looks up. A beat.

JUDGE

Good enough. Hang him.

Gavel.

30

Ext. gallows — day (sepia)[WESTERN / AI]

A noose settles around Chris's neck. Beside him, a YOUNG MAN weeps, terrified. Chris glances over, deadpan.

CHRIS

First time?

Chris's eyes lift to the crowd — and find JESSE. She looks up at him, notebook clutched to her chest. A held beat: she knows now. The idea is loose in the world, and no rope can reach it.

A hood comes down over his head.

BALLAD (V.O.)

They hanged the poor man... but the recipe got away.

CUT TO BLACK. The trap drops — sound only.

END — 30 SHOTS
