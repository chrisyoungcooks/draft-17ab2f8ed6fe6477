# Scene 9 Refinement Notes — Bottom Of The Hour

## Goal
Scene 9 must turn the smoke accumulated in Scene 8 into visible bark, then close the hook loop: one hour of smoke is enough when the surface is ready and the timing is right.

## Iteration 1 — Story Shape
- What worked: opening Scene 9 with the chronometer at 30 minutes gives the hour a physical spine and makes the scene feel like a second phase, not more explanation.
- What failed: the existing graph line, "Not more smoke. Better timing," is true but too abstract. It does not tell the audience what the second half is doing.
- Kept: chronometer at thirty, Chris continuing the same smoke graph, top cutaway of the meat surface, final chronometer at sixty, lid-open reveal.
- Thrown out: any Scene 9 return to fire quality. Scene 8 owns smoke quality and smoke sticking. Scene 9 owns bark building.

## Iteration 2 — Science Accuracy
- What worked: the current scene correctly brings back Arrhenius and concentration.
- What failed: it risks implying Arrhenius is concentration. More accurate: Arrhenius is the temperature effect; dehydration and concentration stack on top by crowding reactive compounds together.
- Kept: rough rule of thumb that reactions around 200°F are about four times faster than near 150°F.
- Added: 400°F is not for cooking the center. The oven already did that. The hot smoker gives enough heat flux to dry and drive the surface through 180°F toward 200°F+ before Mr. Stall can drag it back down.

## Iteration 3 — Buster Scruggs Voice
- What worked: "Top of the hour" and "Bottom of the hour" have the right Western timekeeping flavor.
- What failed: lines like "better timing" and "concentration gives the chemistry more to run into" are accurate but plain.
- Kept: dry, direct statements with a little dust on them.
- Added candidate line shape: "Top of the hour put smoke on the shoulder. Bottom of the hour makes it barbecue."
- Avoided: over-clever metaphors that obscure the science.

## Iteration 4 — Visual Logic
- What worked: the second graph and top-down cutaway can work if they are not duplicates of Scene 8.
- What failed: the current cutaway does not specify enough visible change. We need the audience to see wet sheen disappear, surface temp rise, smoke stain deepen, bark texture tighten, and pepper integrate.
- Kept: top-down cutaway, macro surface, Arrhenius overlay, visible bark timelapse.
- Added: a macro shot where the surface looks nearly landscape-like: water retreats, smoke compounds and meat-surface chemistry concentrate, texture becomes craggy.
- Added: the graph should show three layers at once: surface temp climbing, moisture falling, concentration rising.

## Iteration 5 — Save The Cat Function
- Scene 7 is the "do the work" procedural promise.
- Scene 8 is the "new rule" reveal: smoke has to stick, and Mr. Stall usually prevents that.
- Scene 9 must be the "synthesis payoff": once smoke has stuck, heat plus concentration turn it into bark fast.
- The audience question at the start of Scene 9: "Okay, smoke is on the meat. Why is another half hour enough?"
- The answer at the end: "Because the surface is now hot, drying, concentrated, and reactive. One clean hour was enough because the two halves did different jobs."
- Final loop closure: chronometer hits 60, lid opens, finished pork mirrors Shot 2.
