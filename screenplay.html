Real Barbecue Only Needs 1 Hour of Smoke

The Ballad of a Barbecue Rustler
written & performed by
Chris Young

FADE IN

EXT. RANCH YARD — DAY

Hard noon sun. A desert ranch yard. Chris Young — white hat, whiter shirt — leans on the lid handle of a Big Green Egg smoker, into the lens.

CHRIS

Folks'll tell you great barbecue takes all day. But—

On "But" he hauls the lid up — it wipes the frame, and from black, inside the Egg, the same lid lifts. Hard daylight floods in, cutting through smoke, and an almost-black pork shoulder resolves on the grate — all bark and glow.

CHRIS (V.O.)

this...
this pork shoulder only smoked—

A vintage chronometer fills the foreground, the open Egg soft behind it. The hour races across the dial, then ticks in real time.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

—for one hour.

His thumb comes down on the crown. CLICK. The hand stops dead on sixty.

INT. KITCHEN — DAY

He struts in from the dining room of a rustic adobe ranch house, dusting the smoke off his chest — two taps, and the little cloud hangs behind him in a shaft of light. He squares to the lens, swagger dropping into direct address.

CHRIS

Reverse smoking leaves the hours of smoke'n sweat to those that enjoy 'em.
Which ain't me.
Maybe y'all've heard a me, Chris Young—

On his name, his eyes flick down; his hand drops toward the counter.

Close on a heavy volume on the counter: MODERNIST CUISINE. His hand flips through it.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

—known to some for writin' the book on how cooking really works. 2,438 pages.

The hand pauses, then turns past the last real spread — to a page that does not exist.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Turns out barbecue needed a few more.

Across the black page, a cutaway drawing of the Big Green Egg blooms like ink coming alive. We push in until the page swallows the frame—

Inside the cutaway, a pork shoulder timelapses raw to done: pale meat darkens, bark forms, smoke rolls past. We pull back — the Egg holds its place while ceramic shell and a desert dissolve in around it. Golden hour now, and the smoker stands outdoors.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Unless I grievously misremember, the question we never did ask: How much smoke do you need?

EXT. DESERT — DAY

Chris beside the Egg.

CHRIS

So I measured it.

He draws in the air — and the plot hangs there, drawn in smoke: a long cook crawling flat across the hours.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

Appears for most of it, the smoke's just keepin' itself company.

Then the final climb, banded bright.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

The last hour is all that matters.

He blows the graph apart. It drifts off as plain smoke.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

The sufferin' was never what made barbecue great.

EXT. A TOWN — DUSK

High over a desert main street: storefronts, chimney smoke, mesas beyond. The light drains to blue-black; windows kindle; stars come up.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Reverse smoking can make your barbecue the best in your town—without handin' the smoker your whole day… nor your night neither.

INT. BEDROOM — NIGHT

Chris settles in under a lone lamp.

CHRIS

And I aim t'show you how your oven can do the hard work—

He reaches for the lamp.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

—while you sleep.

CLICK. Black.

OVER BLACK

CHRIS (V.O.)

There's a catch, of course.

INT. KITCHEN — DAY

From black — a sheet of heavy foil SNAPS out of its box, a sharp full-arm pull torn off against the teeth.

The sheet lies stretched taut. In its warped silver: Chris looks back — hat and shoulders, held still.

CHRIS (V.O.)

…you do ever'thing backwards.

A seasoned pork butt LANDS on his reflection. THUD.

In less time than it takes to tell, the foil folds over, seam by seam, then crimps tight.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

We wrap at the start — in this case, a pork butt dressed in nothin' but salt'n pepper, as the good Lord intended.

Close on a probe piercing the foil, sliding deep into the meat.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

And my trusty Predictive Thermometer rides along — keeps me apprised of all the goin's-on—

Inside the oven: the wrapped package slides onto the rack and lands with real weight, the thermometer standing in the roast.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

—as it cooks low'n slow in m'oven while I sleep.

The oven knob clicks past 550… 300… 200…

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

But below two hundred, a lot of ovens just say warm.

The knob lands on WARM. Chris looks from the dial to the camera.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

"Warm." Tells me how a blanket feels, not how long t'cook a shoulder.

Inside the oven, the package sits in the glow. Around the probe, clean readouts fade in: AIR AT PORK 170°F. SURFACE climbing. CORE following.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

My Predictive Thermometer shows the air right around the pork settlin' at about one-seventy.

The surface line rises toward the air line; the core trails behind it.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Wrapped up, the surface climbs to meet it, and the core follows along behind.

Tilt up from a second oven tray — a bare comparison butt, dark with Dalmatian rub — to Chris. Not the dinner; the control case.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

But things go considerable different…

He slaps the bare pork.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

…for a naked butt.

He loads the bare roast into the same oven, apart from the wrapped pork already cooking.

Inside the oven: the naked butt, and a clean readout beside it. AIR AT PORK 170. CORE 138… 139… 140.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Same oven. Same temperature.

The core number stalls far below the air.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

But naked, the pork stalls out.

Time compresses. The air-at-pork line stays boring. The core flattens around 140.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Hours go by.

The core barely moves: 141… 143… 146…

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

And still nowhere near done — just settin' there sweatin', stubborn as a mule.

INT. DINING ROOM — DAY

Chris faces the camera, tight. Below frame, one hand nudges something into place — a tiny mechanical CLICK. The hand settles.

CHRIS

Somethin' in that oven is robbin' this cook blind.
So I built m'self a way t'catch him in the act.

He moves off along the dining table, past a strange apparatus, adjusting a curved mirror a fraction as he goes. We rise with him until the whole rig maps out below — projector, pinhole card, mirror, a steaming mug, second mirror, razor blade, screen: a great dark Z of equipment laid across a dinner table.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

This here is schlieren photography — a 160-year-old optical trick with mirrors, a razor blade—

His hand flips the projector switch.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

—and a very bright light.

The lamp SNAPS on. A hard beam cuts through the dust — and we go with it:

The beam hits a card. A pinhole crushes the room's light down to a single point—

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

—that a pinhole turns into a point—

—the point strikes the first telescope mirror and fires back across the table—

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

—and a mirror reflects back across the table—

—past a graniteware pot pouring coffee, steam blooming up into the beam—

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

—through steam from my hot coffee—

—into the second mirror, which squeezes the light back toward focus—

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

—to a second mirror that refocuses it to a point—

—where a razor blade stands edge-on in the light and cuts the point in half.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

—that's cut in half by a razor blade.

The ride ends at an old-fashioned projection screen. On it: the coffee steam, appearing as bands of light and shadow — alive, climbing.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Wherever the steam bends the light, the blade turns that bend into light and shadow on the screen.

High again: the same map, lit now — light strung between every object on the table. At the far end, the plume shimmers on the screen.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Makin' the invisible visible.

Chris turns from the glowing rig to the camera.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

Now let's aim this into m'oven.

Through the mirrors, inside the oven: the pork sits framed in a soft circular halo of light. Almost ordinary — until the knife-edge snaps in and the image resolves: a plume, turbulent and alive, streaming up off the shoulder.

CHRIS (V.O.)

There he is.
(beat)
Mister Stall.
Known to some as the Plateau. He's got other handles, nicknames, appelations, and cognoments…

The plume keeps rolling off the pork.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

…but he's just water vapor leavin'.
And the heat goin' with it keeps the pork cooler than the oven.

The plume shifts, pulses, and changes.

Back on the table, a vintage book waits: PSYCHROMETRIC TABLES AND CHARTS. Chris's hand enters and flips it open — dense old charts, tiny numbers, sloping humidity lines. Page after page.

CHRIS (V.O.)

You can predict how Mister Stall moves your cookin' temperature around—
—with humidity, airflow, altitude, wet-bulb temperature — the whole entire psy-chro-met-ric arts, bound in a book no sane person keeps by the oven.

Across the table, the open charts fill the foreground. We pan up from the book to Chris.

CHRIS

Or you can relieve him of his only trick.
(beat)
E-vap-o-ra-tion.

Through the mirrors again: the already-wrapped pork. Same oven — but the image is calm now. For a beat it almost looks broken: no plume, just slow convection rippling around the package.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Wrapped, the plume disappears.

Only after that beat, a clean readout fades in: AIR AT PORK 170. PORK SURFACE (UNDER FOIL) 166… 168… 170. CORE climbing slowly.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Now the surface climbs toward the air around it.

The surface line rises smoothly to meet the air; the core follows behind it.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

And once the surface is predictable—
—the cookin' time is predictable.
It's why I built the Combustion Predictive Thermometer: to read the surface, not just the center.

The foil package stays where it has been since the start: already loaded, already cooking, already under control.

INT. KITCHEN — DUSK

Close on the wrapped pork in the oven, the thermometer standing in the roast — then we pull back, and back, until the oven is a small warm rectangle of light in a darkening kitchen.

CHRIS (V.O.)

In m'oven, warm's about one-seventy.
But every oven lies a little — that's no cause for anger nor dismay, just cause t'trust the thermometer at the food, not the number you set.

INT. BEDROOM — NIGHT

The oven's glow becomes a warm bedside lamp. Beneath it, a vintage cookbook waits on the nightstand. Chris's hand reaches in from bed and lifts it; we follow the book back to him, settled under the covers.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Once you know your number, warm stops bein' a guess.
Now the question is how long t'hold it.

He opens to a page marked TIME AT TEMPERATURE.

Over his shoulder: the chapter opens on an engraved portrait plate — a bearded Victorian gentleman, an equation printed beneath him.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Tenderness is not just a final temperature.
It's time at temperature.
For tough cuts like pork shoulder, the useful change is collagen slowly convertin' into gelatin.
I'm not a devious man by nature. But when you mean t'sleep through the hard part, your tactics might gotta be downright Arrhenian.
Which brings this Swede — Svante Arrhenius — into our bedtime story.

On the name, he turns the page, and we push in until the printed spread fills the frame. A loose collagen strand at the base of the axes pulls upward and becomes a curve, drawing itself shallow at first.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Arrhenius worked out that chemical reactions speed up as temperature rises.
But not in a straight line.
Ex-po-nen-tially.

The curve whips upward steep, ink thickening on the upswing. The printed clock beside it squeezes shorter.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Increase the temperature a little bit, and things go a lot faster.
Now if you'll forgive a touch of arithmetic at bedtime: every twenty degrees Fahrenheit you climb, collagen turns to gelatin about 1.8 times faster.

We settle on the margin, where the rule has been printed all along: "1.8x per 20°F." The book knew.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

So once Mister Stall is out of the way, and you know your temperature, Arrhenius gives you the time.

We rest on the finished curve. In the page corner, a tiny printed vignette: a plume boxed behind a foil border.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

And because smoke flavor is chemistry too, he'll be helpin' us again tomorrow.

One curl of ink-smoke rises from the corner, settles onto the curve, and stills — print again. We pull back as he turns to the last page, the one the old cookbook never had: a reverse-smoking timetable.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Once you know what warm means at the surface of your pork, pick the row for your oven.

His finger lands on its row. He marks the page. The book closes — and wider now, he sets it on the nightstand. His hand moves to the lamp.

EXT. RANCH HOUSE — NIGHT

The house sits quiet under the night sky. One bedroom window glows warm.

The light goes out.

A beat of dark house. Then we tilt up, deliberately, to a centered full moon.

EXT. RANCH YARD — DAY

The moon becomes the sun, blazing over the yard. We tilt down to the open Egg. Chris leans in and sets the last piece of kindling.

Against his body, a match strikes. Flame blooms in slow motion.

Inside the Egg: the match touches kindling. Flame catches at the edge, grows, and the first wood blackens as heat shimmer starts to bend the air.

Chris adds wood, chunk on chunk on chunk, onto the burning kindling. Off the catching pile the smoke rises thick, white, dirty.

The pile catches, flames rise — and time compresses: the wood burns down to an even bed of glowing embers.

He rakes the embers level. Off the bare embers, almost no smoke at all — embers are heat, not smoke.

Several large chunks of fresh, dry wood land on the ember bed. White smoke SURGES off them the moment they land.

The ceramic deflector settles in, heavy and deliberate. A metal tray goes in above it. Water pours — a small HISS from the heat below. The grate drops into place. The dome closes over the built fire and empty grate.

The top vent slides to three-quarters shut. Smoke pulses through the gap, still white, still heavy.

The Combustion Engine's dial turns to 400°F. The fan wakes. The fire starts getting measured air.

On the Giant Grill Gauge the needle climbs, steadies at 400 — and off the vent, the smoke still lifts heavy and white. The number is right. The smoke is wrong.

Chris stands facing the Egg, hands still, watching it.

CHRIS

Not yet.

The smoke thins — and turns blue.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

There.

At the work table: black-gloved hands pull open the foil seam. Steam rolls out. The shoulder inside is cooked and tender-looking — but pale, wet, unfinished. The thermometer still stands in the meat, on duty since last night.

CHRIS (V.O.)

Tender clean through — the oven's been at it since yesterday.
But nobody'd call this barbecue.

He lifts the hot shoulder free of the foil.

Back at the Egg — open again, heat haze lifting off the grate. The pork lands on the grill. Smoke moves around the wet shoulder. The lid closes; smoke pulses from the seam, then disappears.

Close on the vintage chronometer, reset to zero. CLICK. The hand starts moving.

He slides the watch into his shirt pocket and turns to the camera.

CHRIS

Clock's runnin'.

He glances toward the Egg, then back.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

Now, some folks'll smoke a shoulder all day and call that patience. I call it a poor use of daylight.

He draws a line of smoke across the air: the hour.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

As for tenderness, the oven settled that business.
What remains is the surface.
And an hour, properly spent, is enough.

He splits the smoke line at thirty minutes. A label ghosts into the first half.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

Top of the hour sticks the smoke.

A first curve climbs from about one-seventy toward one-eighty. The back half of the hour stays a faint dotted trace.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

Bottom of the hour builds the bark.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

And if we're only keepin' one hour of smoke—

He lets the sentence hang and looks past the smoke graph to the Egg.

The Egg alone in the sun, centered and solid, thin blue-gray smoke slipping from the vent.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

—it had better be smoke of good character.

We dolly toward it. The daylight falls away behind it, and the front of the shell dissolves — the whole smoking system laid open in cutaway: embers, fresh wood, deflector, water tray, grate, pork, smoke path, dome.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

And character starts down here.

Down into the firebox. Measured air slips in beneath the ember bed; the embers brighten. Above them, the fresh wood chars and catches with a slow, lazy flame, letting off thin blue smoke.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

The fan in my Combustion Engine deals fresh air to the embers first.
That glow tells you they got their share. Ain't that right, boys?
What's left keeps the fresh wood honest: enough air t'burn clean, not enough t'run wild.
That's the fire bed our hour deserves: dry wood, hot embers, a lazy flame, and thin blue smoke.

Closer: one big chunk, glowing at the surface, smoke pushing out through its cracks. Its front half dissolves away — cool unburned heartwood, a heating band, charred surface, volatiles percolating outward.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

But the wood has character too.
A big chunk heats like a steak: charred and hot outside, cooler in the middle.
The cool middle makes a rougher smoke, and it has t'find its way out through that hot char.
The char knocks off the harsh, leaves the edge — smoke with a little dust on its boots.

Up with the smoke. The flame climbs after it and stops dead at the ceramic plate; the smoke bends around, slides past the water tray — simmering, never past a boil — and rises toward the grate.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Smoke rises, and the flame follows it up — until it can't.
The plate stops the flame; the smoke rides on.
Then the water tray — wet enough it never gets past a boil.
Keeps the bottom from gettin' its britches scorched.
And up top, the shoulder's waitin'.

At the meat surface, a plume of water vapor still lifts off the shoulder while smoke works in from below. Some smoke lands — the next surge of steam sweeps it right back off. Almost nothing builds.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Smoke cain't just bless the meat — has t'earn its keep.
It has to ad-sorb. Stick to the surface.
During the stall, it's swimmin' upstream against that plume of water vapor.
Some lands. The next surge of steam sweeps it right back off.
That's why smoke builds so slow.
Mister Stall don't just stall the cook. He stalls the smoke.

The plume dwindles to almost nothing. The wet shine fades to tacky — and for the first time, the smoke stays: a thin, even stain building across the shoulder.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Now, Mister Stall runs on water. And this shoulder shed most of it overnight in the oven.
So the poor sonofagun shows up plumb out of ammunition — barely a plume left in him.
The surface turns tacky'n the smoke sticks.
That's the secret of reverse smoking: you don't beat the stall. You skip it.

The chronometer hand crosses thirty. His thumb sweeps the watch away.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Half the hour's gone. That was the top: stickin' the smoke on.

Chris turns back to the camera, the Egg behind him.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

We've got smoke on the shoulder now, but smoke settin' on meat ain't barbecue — not yet.
This second half is where we turn that smoke into bark.
The oven already made it tender; all the smoker owes us now is a crust.

As he talks, a little line-art steak sketches itself in smoke beside him: gentle wavy heat lines beneath it — then a hard skillet line flares under it, and the crust edge darkens.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

Puts me in mind of a reverse sear — same trick, near enough.
Cook it low'n gentle all the way through, then finish hot'n fast for the crust.
Only mine don't finish in a skillet — it finishes in smoke.
And the reason a half hour of hot is enough—

The sketch stretches — its lines remold into axes, and a rate curve draws itself steep. He taps the low end: a point lights. He sweeps up to one-ninety: a second point flares, a bracket between them.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

—is our ol' friend Arrhenius again.
A traditional cook spends most of its day down here, where the bark builds slow.
Push the surface up near one-ninety, and the same reactions run about five times faster.
Same chemistry. Same result. Just done hotter.

Top-down into macro over the shoulder, time compressing: the last sheen lifts off, heat shimmer rises, the color deepens, the crust craggs, the pepper darkens in.

CHRIS (V.O.) (CONT'D)

Things have a way of escalatin' in a hurry when the pit runs four hundred.
Its one job now is t'drive the last water out of that tacky surface the smoke stuck to.
As that water leaves, ever'thing left behind packs in tight: smoke, salt, pepper, the sugars in the meat.
Hotter and more concentrated, all at once — that's the moment smoke stops ridin' on the meat and turns into bark.
The color goes dark, the pepper mellows, the smoke turns sweet, and you get a crust you can hear.

Chris looks at the completed graph hanging beside him — then blows it away into the smoker's real smoke.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

So — how much smoke does barbecue really need? Don't need but the one clean hour.
Take the two jobs of barbecue, cook'n crust, and run 'em backwards — bein' the which-a-why I call it reverse smoking.
Tender in the oven overnight, while you're asleep.
Bark on the smoker, one hot hour, while you're awake to enjoy it.
The hard part, you do lyin' down.

The chronometer reaches sixty. CLICK. Stopped.

Chris pockets the watch and sets his hand on the lid. One look to the camera.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

One hour of honest smoke, and a night's sleep behind it.
Let's see what that bought us.

He lifts the lid. Smoke rolls out — and clears on the shoulder: dark, craggy, barked. Unmistakably barbecue. Hold on it.

INT. KITCHEN — DAY

The shoulder LANDS on a heavy cutting board. THUD. Bare hands sink in and tear a whole section free — bark CRACKING, steam pouring.

Very close: fingers shred the section into glistening strands, bark shattering and folding through the meat, steam rising through the backlight. It goes on longer than feels responsible.

Chris, at the counter in hard slats of daylight, lifts a strand and eats. For one beat the showman drops — a real, private reaction. Then, plain, to the camera:

CHRIS

That's some pig!

He gathers the board and turns out of frame with purpose.

INT. DINING ROOM — DAY

Tight on the long table: the board with the shredded-open shoulder settles down into frame. His hands withdraw. The room around it stays out of frame — for all we know, it's supper.

Then the reveal: a frontal three-shot, square across the table — three pitmasters seated in a row against two burning gridded windows, weathered and formidable, the shoulder soft in the bottom of frame between us and them. The room has been occupied all along, and three pairs of eyes are already down on the meat.

A beat. In unison, the eyes come up to the man who brought it.

The reverse at last: Chris on the other side of the table, waist-up, the window light full on him — in the white fit he practically glows. Thumbs hooked in his pockets. Mighty pleased with himself. He does not move.

The trio again. Nobody touches anything. Then the two flanking men turn their heads — not to the meat, to the HARDEST PITMASTER, dead center. His eyes stay on Chris a moment longer. Then he reaches.

High on the shoulder: his hand pulls a piece free. Then the left. Then the right. Bark CRACKS. Steam rises.

They chew. Together. Unreadable.

The left pitmaster tips forward over the table and catches the right pitmaster's eye past the hardest man.

LEFT PITMASTER

That's pretty good.

The right pitmaster leans in to meet the look. Chews. Concedes with a nod.

RIGHT PITMASTER

Real good.

Between them, the hardest man chews on, eyes front. A stone.

The only tight single at the table, and it creeps — a slow push-in through the last of the chew, the dark pier between the windows behind him: the one man at the table without a halo. The chew slows. A flicker he cannot hide. A hard swallow. His eyes come up — dead into the lens.

HARDEST PITMASTER

That's cheatin' barbecue.

The wide at last, withheld through the whole trial: the long table, the three pitmasters in a row against the burning windows, the pork between them, Chris standing at the near edge in silhouette, the white hat rim-lit. The three stare at Chris. He stares back. Nobody moves. The room goes hard.

EXT. THE GALLOWS — DAY

Wide. Chris stands on a buckboard, wrists bound, the noose around his neck, hat still on. A line of condemned men beside him; a crowd below.

He lets out a long sigh.

CHRIS (V.O.)

I guess I shoulda seen this comin'.
M'fellow pitmasters ain't much interested in bein' challenged on the way they do things. Why, I figure that's just the human material.

The young man noosed beside him sobs. Chris turns to him — wry, unhurried, like he has stood on this cart before.

CHRIS (CONT'D)

Mmm. First time?

WEEPING MAN

Huh?

Chris turns front. He does not explain.

A black bag drops over his head.

OVER BLACK

The cart goes. The fall. The rope SNAPS taut.

The crowd ERUPTS in a cheer.

THE END
